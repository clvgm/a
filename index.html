#NC10-MAT21-20220101-S01-Q08
A Round Table cover has 6 equal designs as shown in the figure.
If radius of the cover is 28cm; Find the cost of making the designs at the rate of $₹0.35 / cm^2$.
(Use $\sqrt{3} = 1.7$)
As the table cloth has 6 equal designs, the total area of the designs can be obtained by subtracting the area of hexagon from the area of the circle.
By drawing the diagonals of this hexagon, it can be divided into 6 equilateral triangles of side equal to radius of the table cloth.
Let's now calculate the area of the design,
$$ \begin{align} \text{Area(Design)} &= \text{Area(Circle)} - \text{Area(Hexagon)}\\ &= \text{Area(Circle)} - 6\times\text{Area(}\Delta\text{OAB)}\\ &= \text{Area(Circle)} - 6\times\text{Area(Equilateral Triangle)}\\ \text{Area(Design)} &= \left(\pi r^2\right) - \left(6\times\dfrac{\sqrt 3 a^2}{4}\right)\\ \end{align} $$
As r=28cm and a=r; Hence a=28cm
$$ \begin{align} \text{Area(Design)} &= \left(\pi r^2\right) - \left(6\times\dfrac{\sqrt 3 a^2}{4}\right)\\ &= \left(\dfrac{22}{7} (28)^2\right) - \left(6\times\dfrac{\sqrt 3 (28)^2}{4}\right)\\ &= \left(\dfrac{22}{7} (28\times 28)\right) - \left(6\times\dfrac{\sqrt 3 (28\times 28)}{4}\right)\\ &= \left(\dfrac{22\times 28\times 28}{7} \right) - \left(\dfrac{6\times 1.7 \times 28\times 28}{4}\right)\\ \require{cancel} &= \left(\dfrac{22\times 28\times \cancelto{4}{28}}{\cancelto{1}{7}} \right) - \left(\dfrac{6\times 1.7 \times 28\times \cancelto{7}{28}}{\cancelto{1}{4}}\right)\\ &= \left(22\times 28\times 4 \right) - \left(6\times 1.7 \times 28\times 7\right)\\ &= 28\times \left[\left(22\times 4 \right) - \left(6\times 1.7 \times 7\right)\right]\\ &= 28\times \left[\left(88 \right) - \left(42\times 1.7\right)\right]\\ &= 28\times \left[88 - 71.4\right]\\ &= 28\times \left[16.6\right]\\ \therefore \text{Area(Design)}&= 464.8 \text{cm}^2 \end{align} $$
Now Rate of Making design is $₹0.35 / cm^2$; Hence the total cost of making the design will be,
$$ \begin{align} \text{Cost(Design)} &= \text{Rate} \times Area(Design)\\ &= 0.35 \times 464.8\\ &= \text{ ₹}162.68\\ \therefore \text{Cost(Design)} &\approx \text{ ₹}163 \end{align} $$
Hence the total cost of making the design on the circular table cloth is ₹163.

As $14cm$ is the side of the outer square, and as there are two circles within the square,

#NC10-MAT21-20220101-S01-Q10
The area of an equilateral triangle $\Delta\text{ABC}$ is $17320.5 cm^2$. With each vertex of the triangle as centre, a circle is drawn with radius equal to half the length of the side of the triangle.
Find the area of the shaded region (in green).
(Use $\pi = 3.14$ and $\sqrt{3} = 1.73205$)
As area of the equilateral triangle is given, we can calculate its side. If we know its side then we can calculate the radii of the sectors.
$$ \begin{align} Area(Equilateral Triangle) &= \dfrac{\sqrt{3}a^2}{4}\\\\ 17320.5 &=\dfrac{1.73205 a^2}{4}\\\\ 17320.5 &=\dfrac{1.73205}{4}\times a^2\\\\ 17320.5 \times \dfrac{4}{1.73205} &= a^2\\\\ \dfrac{17320.5\times 4}{1.73205} &= a^2\\\\ \dfrac{1.73205\times 10000\times 4}{1.73205} &= a^2\\\\ \require{cancel}\dfrac{\cancelto{1}{1.73205}\times 10000\times 4}{\cancelto{1}{1.73205}} &= a^2\\\\ \dfrac{10000\times 4}{1} &= a^2\\\\ \sqrt{10000\times 4} &= a\\\\ \sqrt{10000}\times \sqrt{4} &= a\\\\ 100\times 2 &= a\\\\ \therefore 200\text{ cm} &= a \end{align} $$
Radius of the circle is given to be half of the side of the equilateral triangle. Hence,
$$ \begin{align} r &= \dfrac{a}{2}\\ r &= \dfrac{200}{2}\\ \require{cancel}r &= \dfrac{\cancelto{100}{200}}{\cancelto{1}{2}}\\ \therefore r &= 100\text{ cm} \end{align} $$
As the triangle is equilateral, the angle of the sectors will be $60^\circ$
$$ \begin{align} Area(Shaded) &= Area(Triangle) - Area(All Sectors)\\ Area(Shaded) &= Area(Triangle) - 3 \left(Area(Sector)\right)\\ Area(Shaded) &= 17320.5 - 3 \left(\dfrac{\theta}{360^\circ}\times \pi r^2\right)\\ &= 17320.5 - 3 \left(\dfrac{60^\circ}{360^\circ}\times 3.14 \times 100^2\right)\\ \require{cancel}&= 17320.5 - \left(3 \times \dfrac{\cancelto{1}{60^\circ}}{\cancelto{6}{360^\circ}}\times 3.14 \times 100\times 100\right)\\ &= 17320.5 - \left(3\times \dfrac{1}{6}\times 3.14 \times 100\times 100\right)\\ \require{cancel}&= 17320.5 - \dfrac{\cancelto{1}{3}\times 1 \times 314 \times 100}{\cancelto{2}{6}}\\ \require{cancel}&= 17320.5 - \dfrac{ 1 \times \cancelto{157}{314} \times 100}{\cancelto{1}{2}}\\ &= 17320.5 - 15700\\ \therefore Area(Shaded) &= 1620.5\text{ cm}^2\\ \end{align} $$

If the vertices form an isosceles triangle then the lengths of any two sides must be equal.
Let the vertices be $A(5,-2)$, $B(6,4)$ & $C(7,-2)$
$$ \begin{align} A(5,-2) &\text{ ; } B(6,4)\\ Distance &= \sqrt{\left(x_2-x_1\right)^2+\left(y_2-y_1\right)^2}\\ AB &= \sqrt{\left((6)-(5)\right)^2+\left((4)-(-2)\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+6-5\right)^2+\left(+4+2\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+1\right)^2+\left(+6\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{1+36}\\ \therefore AB &= \sqrt{37}\text{ units} \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} B(6,4) &\text{ ; } C(7,-2)\\ Distance &= \sqrt{\left(x_2-x_1\right)^2+\left(y_2-y_1\right)^2}\\ AB &= \sqrt{\left((7)-(6)\right)^2+\left((-2)-(4)\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+7-6\right)^2+\left(-2-4\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+1\right)^2+\left(-6\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{1+36}\\ \therefore AB &= \sqrt{37}\text{ units} \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} C(7,-2) &\text{ ; } A(5,-2)\\ Distance &= \sqrt{\left(x_2-x_1\right)^2+\left(y_2-y_1\right)^2}\\ AB &= \sqrt{\left((5)-(7)\right)^2+\left((-2)-(-2)\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+5-7\right)^2+\left(-2+2\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{\left(+2\right)^2+\left(0\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{4+0}\\ &= \sqrt{4}\\ \therefore AB &= 2\text{ units} \end{align} $$

As the lengths of the Sides AB & BC are the same, hence the triangle formed by the given vertices is Isosceles.

Find the area of the triangle whose vertices are: $(2,3)$, $(-1,0)$, $(2,-4)$

$$ \begin{align} \sin{A}&=\dfrac{3}{4}\\\\ \dfrac{Opp}{Hyp}&=\dfrac{3}{4}\\\\ \dfrac{BC}{AC}&=\dfrac{3}{4}\\\\ \text{By Compari} &\text{sion, we get,}\\\\ \therefore BC &= 3\\\therefore AC &= 4 \end{align} $$
By Pythagoras' Theorem, we get,
$$ \begin{align} AB^2 + BC^2 &= AC^2\\\\ AB^2 + 3^2 &= 4^2\\\\ AB^2 &= 4^2 - 3^2\\\\ AB^2 &= 16 - 9\\\\ AB^2 &= 7\\\\ \therefore AB &= \sqrt 7 \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} \cos{A}&=\dfrac{adj}{hyp}\\\\ \cos{A}&=\dfrac{AB}{AC}\\\\ \therefore \cos{A}&=\dfrac{\sqrt 7}{4} \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} \tan{A}&=\dfrac{opp}{adj}\\\\ \tan{A}&=\dfrac{BC}{AB}\\\\ \therefore \tan{A}&=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt 7} \end{align} $$

Find the value of
$$\dfrac{5\cos^2{60^\circ} + 4\sec^2{30^\circ} - \tan^2{45^\circ}}{\sin^2{30^\circ}+\cos^2{30}}$$
$$ \begin{align} Given &= \dfrac{5\cos^2{60^\circ} + 4\sec^2{30^\circ} - \tan^2{45^\circ}}{\sin^2{30^\circ}+\cos^2{30}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{5\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 + 4\left(\dfrac{2}{\sqrt 3}\right)^2 - (1)^2}{\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^2+\left(\dfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}\right)^2}\\\\ &= \dfrac{5\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right) + 4\left(\dfrac{4}{3}\right) - (1)}{\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)+\left(\dfrac{3}{4}\right)}\\\\ &= \dfrac{\dfrac{5}{4} + \dfrac{16}{3} - \dfrac{1}{1}}{\dfrac{1}{4}+\dfrac{3}{4}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{\dfrac{5^{\times 3}}{4_{\times 3}} + \dfrac{16^{\times 4}}{3_{\times 4}} - \dfrac{1^{\times 12}}{1_{\times 12}}}{\dfrac{1+3}{4}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{\dfrac{15+64-12}{12}}{\dfrac{4}{4}}\\\\ \require{cancel}&= \dfrac{\dfrac{15+64-12}{12}}{\dfrac{\cancelto{1}{4}}{\cancelto{1}{4}}}\\\\ \require{cancel}&= \dfrac{\dfrac{67}{12}}{\dfrac{1}{1}}\\\\ &=\dfrac{67}{12}\div \dfrac{1}{1}\\\\ &=\dfrac{67}{12}\times \dfrac{1}{1}\\\\ &=\dfrac{67}{12} \end{align} $$

Find the value of
$$2\tan^2{45^\circ} + \cos^2{30^\circ} - \sin^2{30^\circ}$$
$$ \begin{align} Given &= 2\tan^2{45^\circ} + \cos^2{30^\circ} - \sin^2{30^\circ}\\\\ &=2(1)^2 + \left(\dfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^2\\\\ &=2(1) + \left(\dfrac{3}{4}\right) - \left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)\\\\ &=2 + \dfrac{3 -1}{4}\\\\ &=2 + \dfrac{2}{4}\\\\ \require{cancel}&=2 + \dfrac{\cancelto{1}{2}}{\cancelto{2}{4}}\\\\ &=2 + \dfrac{1}{2}\\\\ &=\dfrac{2^{\times 2}}{1_{\times 2}} + \dfrac{1}{2}\\\\ &=\dfrac{4}{2} + \dfrac{1}{2}\\\\ &=\dfrac{4 + 1}{2}\\\\ &=\dfrac{5}{2} \end{align} $$

Find A & B, if $\tan{(A+B)} = \sqrt{3}$ and $\tan{(A-B)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$;
where $0^\circ \lt A+B \le=90^\circ$ & $A>B$.
Given,
$$ \begin{align} \tan{(A+B)} &= \sqrt{3}\\\\ \tan{(A+B)} &= \tan{60^\circ}\\\\ \text{By Comparing both }&\text{sides, we get,}\\\\ A+B &= 60^\circ \tag{Eq. 1} \end{align} $$
Similarly,
$$ \begin{align} \tan{(A-B)} &= \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\\\\ \tan{(A-B)} &= \tan{30^\circ}\\\\ \text{By Comparing both }&\text{sides, we get,}\\\\ A-B &= 30^\circ \tag{Eq. 1} \end{align} $$
On solving Eq.1 and Eq.2 by Elimination method we get,
$$ \begin{align} A+\cancel{B} &= 60^\circ\\ A-\cancel{B} &= 30^\circ\\ \hline 2A &=90^\circ\\\\ A &=\dfrac{90^\circ}{2}\\\\ A &=\dfrac{\cancelto{45^\circ}{90^\circ}}{\cancelto{1}{2}}\\\\ \therefore A &=45^\circ \end{align} $$
Substituting the value of A in Eq.1, we get,
$$ \begin{align} A+B &= 60^\circ\\\\ 45^\circ+B &= 60^\circ\\\\ B &= 60^\circ-45^\circ\\\\ \therefore B &=15^\circ \end{align} $$

Find the value of
$\dfrac{\sin{18^\circ}}{\cos{72^\circ}}$
$$ \begin{align} Given &= \dfrac{\sin{18^\circ}}{\cos{72^\circ}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{\cos{90^\circ - 18^\circ}}{\cos{72^\circ}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{\cos{72^\circ}}{\cos{72^\circ}}\\\\ \require{cancel}&= \dfrac{\cancelto{1}{\cos{72^\circ}}}{\cancelto{1}{\cos{72^\circ}}}\\\\ &= \dfrac{1}{1}\\\\ &= 1 \end{align} $$

Show that
$\tan{48^\circ}\tan{23^\circ}\tan{42^\circ}\tan{67^\circ} = 1$
$$ \begin{align} \text{LHS} &= \tan{48^\circ}\tan{23^\circ}\tan{42^\circ}\tan{67^\circ}\\\\ &= \tan{48^\circ}\tan{42^\circ}\tan{23^\circ}\tan{67^\circ}\\\\ &= \tan{48^\circ}\cot{(90^\circ - 42^\circ)}\tan{23^\circ}\cot{(90^\circ - 67^\circ)}\\\\ &= \tan{48^\circ}\cot{48^\circ}\tan{23^\circ}\cot{23^\circ}\\\\ &= \tan{48^\circ}\dfrac{1}{\tan{48^\circ}}\tan{23^\circ}\dfrac{1}{\tan{23^\circ}}\\\\ \require{cancel}&= \cancelto{1}{\tan{48^\circ}}\times\dfrac{1}{\cancelto{1}{\tan{48^\circ}}}\times\cancelto{1}{\tan{23^\circ}}\times\dfrac{1}{\cancelto{1}{\tan{23^\circ}}}\\\\ &= 1\times\dfrac{1}{1}\times1\times\dfrac{1}{1}\\\\ &= 1\times 1\times 1\times 1\\\\ &= 1 \end{align} $$

Express $\sin{A}$, $\sec{A}$ & $\tan{A}$ in terms of $\cot{A}$.
$$ \begin{align} \DeclareMathOperator{cosec}{cosec} \cosec^2{A} &= \cot^2{A}+1 \\\\ \dfrac{1}{\sin^2{A}} &= \cot^2{A}+1\\\\ \dfrac{1}{\sin^2{A}} &= \dfrac{\cot^2{A}+1}{1}\\\\ \dfrac{\sin^2{A}}{1} &= \dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}+1}\\\\ \sin^2{A} &= \dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}+1}\\\\ \sin{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}+1}} \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} \sec^2{A} &= \tan^2{A}+1 \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\tan^2{A}+1} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}}+1} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}}+\dfrac{1}{1}} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}}+\dfrac{1^{\times\cot^2{A}}}{1_{\times\cot^2{A}}}} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{\cot^2{A}}+\dfrac{\cot^2{A}}{\cot^2{A}}} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \sqrt{\dfrac{1+\cot^2{A}}{\cot^2{A}}} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \dfrac{\sqrt{1+\cot^2{A}}}{\sqrt{\cot^2{A}}} \\\\ \sec{A} &= \dfrac{\sqrt{1+\cot^2{A}}}{\cot{A}} \end{align} $$
$$ \begin{align} \tan{A} &= \dfrac{1}{\cot{A}} \end{align} $$
